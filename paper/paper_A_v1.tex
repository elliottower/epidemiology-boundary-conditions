\begin{abstract}
Mendelian randomization (MR) estimates often vary across ancestry groups and cohorts, raising the question of which causal effects are transportable and which are stratum-specific. We apply sheaf $Q$ tests---per-edge heterogeneity tests derived from sheaf cohomology---to classify MR pairs as transportable or non-transportable based on the first cohomology group $H^1$.

On scalar effect estimates, we prove that the sheaf $Q$ test statistic reduces algebraically to Cochran's $Q$ via the $W$-weighted Schur complement, establishing that the method's value lies in its per-edge and classification extensions rather than the scalar base case. Simulation experiments on MS- and AD-calibrated structural equation models demonstrate that per-edge sheaf $Q$ tests recover planted DAG structure invisible to global tests ($p < 10^{-300}$ vs.\ $p = 0.659$), with the $H^1$ classifier achieving a three-order-of-magnitude bimodal gap between transportable and stratum-specific mechanism--modifier pairs.

On 61 published MR pairs across five clinical domains (MS, AD, cardiometabolic disease, cancer, psychiatric disorders), the $H^1$ classifier achieves 85.2\% accuracy [75.4\%, 93.4\%] at $\alpha = 0.05$, with zero false positives across all domains and threshold choices. All nine misclassifications are false negatives on underpowered pairs (power $< 0.38$; correctly detected pairs average power $0.89$). Cardiometabolic pairs achieve 100\% accuracy (18/18). Per-edge sheaf $Q$ tests on ADNI longitudinal data recover a three-way DAG classification---mechanism-switching, mediator dose-response, and stable bypass edges---richer than the simulation's binary prediction. Heterogeneity across ancestry strata reflects a composite of causal effect modification, linkage disequilibrium, and instrument validity; the classifier tests exchangeability, not effect modification in isolation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ==============================================================

Mendelian randomization estimates often differ across ancestry groups, cohorts, and patient strata.
Some differences reflect genuine causal effect modification---the biological effect truly varies across populations---while others arise from linkage disequilibrium patterns, allele frequencies, or instrument validity.
Determining which effects generalize across populations and which are stratum-specific is central to evidence synthesis in genetic epidemiology.

Cochran's $Q$ is the standard test for heterogeneity across strata.
Applied to a causal DAG with multiple edges, however, a global heterogeneity test averages variance across edges with different biological roles, diluting edge-specific signal.
In simulation, we show that a global Frobenius norm test returns $p = 0.659$ on a DAG where mechanism edges have $1000\times$ higher variance than downstream edges---structure that per-edge tests recover with $p < 10^{-300}$.

Sheaf consistency testing provides a natural framework for per-edge heterogeneity assessment.
A sheaf on a network assigns local data (\emph{stalks}) to each vertex and measures disagreement through the first cohomology group $H^1$ \citep{robinson2014topological, curry2014sheaves}.
On scalar stalks with pairwise-difference restriction maps, the sheaf Laplacian test statistic reduces algebraically to Cochran's $Q$ (Appendix~\ref{app:sheaf-cochran}).
The per-edge extension---testing each causal relationship independently across strata---has no standard meta-analytic counterpart and enables both DAG structure recovery and transportability classification.

We apply this framework to MR transportability assessment and causal DAG adjudication, with simulation experiments on MS- and AD-calibrated structural equation models followed by real-data validation on published epidemiological estimates across five clinical domains.
Our approach is two-tiered: simulation experiments with planted structure demonstrate \emph{existence and consistency} of each separation, while real-data validation on published epidemiological estimates tests whether these separations survive uncontrolled effect sizes and small stratum counts (\S\ref{sec:realdata}).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Algebraic reduction (Appendix~\ref{app:sheaf-cochran}).} On scalar stalks, the sheaf Laplacian test statistic is \emph{exactly} Cochran's $Q$---not approximately, but algebraically via the $W$-weighted Schur complement. This reduction establishes that the method's value lies in the per-edge and classification extensions, not the scalar case.
\item \textbf{Per-edge DAG adjudication (\S\ref{sec:results}).} Per-edge sheaf $Q$ tests recover planted DAG structure that global tests miss ($p < 10^{-300}$ vs.\ $p = 0.659$), and $H^1$ effect-modifier classification separates transportable from stratum-specific pairs with a three-order-of-magnitude gap.
\item \textbf{Real-data validation (\S\ref{sec:realdata}).} $H^1$ classifier on 61 published MR pairs across 5 clinical domains: 85.2\% accuracy with zero false positives, all failures explained by power.
\item \textbf{ADNI DAG recovery (\S\ref{sec:realdata-dag}).} Per-edge tests on published ADNI longitudinal data---where structure is not planted---recover a three-way classification richer than the simulation prediction.
\item \textbf{Power analysis (\S\ref{sec:realdata-power}).} All nine misclassifications are false negatives with power $< 0.38$; correctly detected pairs have power $> 0.62$, with a bimodal gap between the two groups.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
%% ==============================================================

\subsection{Sheaf consistency testing}
\label{sec:meth-sheaf}

A sheaf on a network $G = (V, E)$ assigns local data (\emph{stalks}) to each vertex and requires that overlapping assignments agree on shared edges via \emph{restriction maps}.
When they disagree, the obstruction lives in the first cohomology group $H^1$, which measures the failure of global consistency \citep{curry2014sheaves, robinson2014topological}.

\paragraph{Scalar stalks.}
For scalar stalks (effect estimates $\hat\beta_k$ with standard errors $\mathrm{se}_k$) and pairwise-difference restriction maps on a complete graph of $K$ sites, the sheaf Laplacian test statistic is
\begin{equation}
    Q = \mathbf{o}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{o}, \quad
    \mathbf{o} = D_0 \boldsymbol{\hat\beta}, \quad
    \Sigma = D_0 \operatorname{diag}(\mathrm{se}^2) D_0^\top,
    \label{eq:sheaf-Q}
\end{equation}
where $D_0$ is the signed incidence matrix and $Q \sim \chi^2_{\mathrm{rank}(D_0)}$ under the null.

\paragraph{Per-edge testing.}
For a causal DAG estimated in $S$ strata, each edge is tested independently:
$Q^{(e)} = \mathbf{z}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{z}$ with $z_{ij} = \hat\beta_i^{(e)} - \hat\beta_j^{(e)}$, under $Q^{(e)} \sim \chi^2_{S-1}$.
Bonferroni correction controls the family-wise error rate.

\paragraph{$H^1$ effect-modifier classification.}
For a mechanism--modifier pair, patients are stratified by the modifier and the causal effect estimated within each stratum.
The sheaf $Q$ test classifies the pair as transportable ($Q$ non-significant, $H^1 \approx 0$) or stratum-specific ($Q$ significant, $H^1 \neq 0$).
Sample size per stratum is calibrated to expected interaction strength.

\subsection{Confound detection}
\label{sec:meth-confound}

\paragraph{Partial correlation collapse.}
For each biomarker $j$, we compute raw Pearson correlation, partial correlation (conditioning on all other covariates), and intensive margin correlation (conditioning on treatment).
Confounded associations should collapse under partial conditioning.

\paragraph{Bracket-norm confound audit.}
Following the confound-audit framework of \citet{tower2026bracket}, we test whether geometric metrics survive after controlling for a mundane confound. For multi-site imaging, confound leakage is $\Delta = (R^2_{\mathrm{metric}} - R^2_{\mathrm{unique}}) / R^2_{\mathrm{metric}}$, where $R^2_{\mathrm{unique}} = R^2_{\mathrm{both}} - R^2_{\mathrm{acq}}$ is disease-severity variance unexplained by acquisition parameters.

\subsection{Per-edge sheaf $Q$ tests recover DAG structure}
\label{sec:res-dag}

\paragraph{Global tests miss edge-specific structure.}
A 3-node MS DAG (inflammation $\leftrightarrow$ degeneration $\to$ disability) is estimated in 8 disease strata.
The feedback edges vary dramatically: early RRMS shows dominant infl$\to$degen ($0.404$) with negligible reverse flow ($-0.001$), while PPMS shows the opposite ($-0.008$ and $0.385$).
Disability edges remain stable (variance $\sim\!100\times$ smaller).

A global Frobenius norm test averages these variances and produces $p = 0.659$ (non-significant).
Per-edge sheaf $Q$ tests recover the true structure (Table~\ref{tab:dag}). With four edges tested, the Bonferroni-corrected threshold is $\alpha_{\mathrm{corr}} = 0.05/4 = 0.0125$; both mechanism edges survive correction ($p < 10^{-300}$), while both downstream edges remain non-significant ($p > 0.18$).

\subsection{Confound detection}
\label{sec:res-confound}

\paragraph{Partial correlation under complete confounding.}
With 10 real and 10 confounded biomarkers ($n = 3{,}000$), partial correlation achieves AUROC $= 1.000$ at all sample sizes ($n = 200$--$5{,}000$) with zero variance.
Confounded biomarkers collapse to partial correlations of $0.001$--$0.033$; real biomarkers retain $0.61$--$0.76$.
This perfect separation is a property of complete confounding (shared-cause pathways that conditioning fully removes) and would degrade under partial confounding or unobserved confounders.

\section{Real-data validation}
\label{sec:realdata}
%% ==============================================================

The simulation experiments establish that per-edge sheaf $Q$ tests and $H^1$ classification work under controlled data-generating processes. We now test whether the positive results hold on published epidemiological estimates, where effect sizes, standard errors, and stratum counts are determined by real studies.

\subsection{$H^1$ classification on published MR estimates}
\label{sec:realdata-h1}

We apply the sheaf $Q$ test to 61 exposure--outcome pairs with published multi-ancestry or multi-cohort genetic effect estimates across five clinical domains: MS (9 pairs), AD (17), cardiometabolic disease (18), cancer (11), and psychiatric disorders (6). Each pair is classified as transportable ($Q$ non-significant) or non-transportable ($Q$ significant) at $\alpha = 0.05$. The expansion from the original 17 neuro-specific pairs to 61 pairs spanning five domains tests whether the $H^1$ classification generalizes beyond neurological diseases.

As established in Appendix~\ref{app:sheaf-cochran}, on scalar effect estimates the sheaf $Q$ test is algebraically identical to Cochran's $Q$---the same statistic the MR field already uses for instrument heterogeneity.
The contribution here is a new \emph{application} of an established test: we apply the established heterogeneity $Q$ across ancestry strata and interpret significant heterogeneity as an $H^1$ obstruction to transportability.
An important caveat: heterogeneity across ancestry groups reflects not only causal effect modification but also differences in linkage disequilibrium, allele frequency, instrument validity, and demographic structure.
A high $Q$ for HLA-DRB1$\to$MS risk across ancestries is expected from LD alone.
The classification therefore tests a composite hypothesis---that strata-level estimates are exchangeable---rather than isolating causal effect modification from these confounded sources of heterogeneity.

\subsection{Power analysis}
\label{sec:realdata-power}

We compute the $Q$ test's statistical power for each of the 61 pairs using the noncentral $\chi^2$ distribution at the observed between-stratum variance $\tau^2$. All nine misclassifications have power $< 0.38$ (Table~\ref{tab:realdata-power}). No pair with power $\geq 0.50$ is misclassified. The power distribution is bimodal: correctly detected non-transport pairs cluster above $0.62$ (mean $0.89$), while misclassified pairs cluster below $0.38$ (mean $0.17$), with no pairs in the intermediate range. The nine underpowered false negatives would require 6--8 strata (vs.\ the available 3--4) to reach 80\% power at the same between-stratum variance. Note that power is computed at the \emph{observed} $\tau^2$, which is the standard approach for post-hoc power analysis but means the power values are estimated, not pre-specified; the bimodal gap between the two groups ($0.38$ vs.\ $0.62$ with no pairs in between) is the more robust diagnostic than any individual power value.

The power distribution is bimodal: correctly detected non-transport pairs cluster above 0.62 (with APOE4$\to$AD reaching ${\sim}1.00$ and sex$\times$tau reaching $0.86$), while misclassified pairs cluster below 0.38. The cardiometabolic domain has the highest detection power because ancestry-stratified GWAS for cardiometabolic traits have the largest sample sizes, producing well-separated $Q$ values.

The 100\% specificity is expected rather than surprising: under the null (homogeneous effects), $Q$ follows a $\chi^2$ distribution whose calibration does not depend on power, so transport pairs produce $Q < 1$ with high probability regardless of sample size.
The informative comparison is therefore on the sensitivity side: with 3--4 strata, the $Q$ test detects heterogeneity only when between-stratum variance is large relative to within-stratum SE.
With 8+ strata (as in simulation), the same effect sizes yield power $> 0.95$; the gap between 85.2\% accuracy (3--4 strata) and $> 99\%$ in simulation is entirely a power effect.
The expansion from 17 to 61 pairs confirms this: false negatives are explained by power (all nine have power $< 0.38$), and false positives do not occur at any threshold up to $\alpha = 0.30$.

\paragraph{Minimum detectable heterogeneity.}
With 3--4 strata (our data structure), the $Q$ test achieves 80\% power at between-stratum SD $\tau_{80} = 0.214$ for $K = 3$ ($\tau_{80}/\mathrm{SE} = 3.57$) and $\tau_{80} = 0.153$ for $K = 4$ ($\tau_{80}/\mathrm{SE} = 2.55$), computed via 2{,}000-replicate simulation at the empirical median within-stratum SE of 0.06.
All nine misclassified pairs have $\tau < \tau_{80}$ for their respective $K$: their between-stratum heterogeneity falls below the detection floor, confirming that misclassification is entirely a power phenomenon.
Among the 13 correctly detected non-transport pairs, 6 have $\tau > \tau_{80}$ (including APOE4$\to$AD at $\tau = 0.344$, the strongest), while 7 are detected despite $\tau < \tau_{80}$ because their within-stratum SEs are smaller than the median---producing large $Q$ even at moderate $\tau$.
The practical implication: detecting non-transportability at 80\% power requires between-stratum effect variation $\geq 2.5$--$3.6\times$ the typical within-stratum SE, placing a hard floor on detectable heterogeneity from summary statistics with few strata.

\subsection{Stratum-level robustness}
\label{sec:realdata-stratum-robustness}

\paragraph{Scope of sensitivity analyses.}
Standard MR sensitivity analyses---MR-Egger, weighted median, weighted mode, Steiger filtering---operate at the level of individual SNP instruments (typically dozens per exposure--outcome pair) and test pleiotropy, outlier robustness, and causal directionality of those instruments.
The $H^1$ classifier operates at the stratum level (3--4 ancestry groups per pair), testing whether the \emph{composite estimate}---already aggregated across instruments within each stratum---is exchangeable across populations.
The two levels answer different questions: MR-Egger tests whether individual instruments satisfy the exclusion restriction; the $Q$ test on stratum estimates tests whether the causal effect (or its confounds) varies across populations.
We therefore report three stratum-level robustness checks rather than instrument-level pleiotropy analyses, which would require individual-level GWAS data.

\paragraph{Exclusion of approximate strata.}
Of the 61 pairs, 58 contain at least one stratum with calibrated or approximate effect estimates (imputed from related studies or cross-ancestry meta-analyses); only 3 pairs have fully exact, directly published estimates for all strata (APOE4$\to$AD, sex$\times$tau spread, age$\times$lecanemab).
On the 3 exact-only pairs, accuracy is 67\% (2/3; the one failure is age$\times$lecanemab, an underpowered false negative with power $= 0.17$).
On the 58 approximate-containing pairs, accuracy is 86.2\% (50/58), marginally higher than the overall 85.2\%.
The small exact-only sample precludes statistical comparison, but the result is directionally reassuring: approximate strata do not inflate false positive rates (specificity remains 100\% on both subsets), and the one exact-only failure is explained by power, not by data quality.

\paragraph{Leave-one-out stability.}
For each pair, we remove each stratum in turn and recompute the $Q$ test.
A pair is \emph{stable} if classification is unchanged regardless of which stratum is dropped.
Across all 61 pairs, 52 (85.2\%) are fully stable (Table~\ref{tab:loo}).
All 39 transport pairs are stable (dropping any stratum leaves $Q < 1$, $p > 0.5$).
The 9 unstable pairs are all non-transport: 6 are correctly classified but flip when the single most heterogeneous stratum is removed (e.g., HLA-DRB1 depends on the EUR IMSGC stratum, which has the narrowest SE and most extreme effect); 3 are already misclassified false negatives that become correctly classified when a particular stratum is dropped.
In every unstable case, exactly one stratum drives the classification, reflecting the fundamental power limitation of 3--4-stratum meta-analysis.

\paragraph{Fixed-effects vs.\ random-effects comparison.}
DerSimonian--Laird random-effects pooled estimates differ negligibly from fixed-effects estimates: mean $|\hat\beta_{\mathrm{FE}} - \hat\beta_{\mathrm{RE}}| = 0.012$, maximum $0.202$ (HLA-DRB1, driven by its extreme between-stratum heterogeneity, $I^2 = 87\%$).
For transport pairs ($\hat\tau^2 = 0$), random-effects and fixed-effects are identical by construction.
For non-transport pairs, the random-effects standard error is on average $1.5\times$ larger than fixed-effects (maximum $7.7\times$ for HLA-DRB1), reflecting genuine between-stratum variance that the fixed-effects model underestimates.
The $Q$ test's classification is invariant to the pooling model because it tests heterogeneity (the variance of stratum estimates), not the pooled point estimate; the random-effects comparison confirms that the detected heterogeneity is consistent with the DerSimonian--Laird variance decomposition.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ==============================================================

\subsection{Cross-domain evidence}

The AD simulation tests whether the per-edge sheaf $Q$ results are artifacts of MS-specific simulation design.
The same structural signature---heterogeneous mechanism edges, homogeneous downstream edges, $H^1 \neq 0$, clean bimodal transport/non-transport separation---emerges from simulations of a disease with distinct etiology (amyloid cascade vs.\ autoimmune demyelination), distinct genetic architecture (APOE4 vs.\ HLA), distinct biomarker modalities (PET/plasma vs.\ MRI/serum), and distinct clinical endpoints (cognitive decline vs.\ physical disability).

A transparency note: the MS and AD simulations use the same \emph{structural pattern} of coefficient variation (mechanism edges with $100\times$ higher variance than downstream edges; $\gamma$ values for $H^1$ classification matched across domains; see Appendix~\ref{app:sim-params}, Tables~\ref{tab:ms-sem} and~\ref{tab:ad-sem}).
The simulation ``replication'' therefore demonstrates consistency under the same data-generating structure with different labels---it confirms that the pipeline produces correct output on inputs with the same statistical properties, which is a necessary sanity check but does not constitute cross-domain generalization.

The real-data validation (\S\ref{sec:realdata}) provides the actual evidence of cross-domain generalization because the effect sizes, stratum counts, and between-stratum variance are determined by published studies, not simulation parameters.
The expansion from 17 neuro-specific pairs to 61 pairs across five clinical domains---with cardiometabolic pairs achieving 100\% accuracy (18/18) and cancer pairs at 91\% (10/11)---demonstrates that the $H^1$ classification operates across diseases with fundamentally different causal architectures, genetic bases, and epidemiological study designs.

\subsection{Connection to transportability theory}

The $H^1$ classifier tests whether effect estimates are exchangeable across strata---what formal transportability theory frames as determining whether a causal quantity can be recovered across domains using selection diagrams and do-calculus transport formulae.
On scalar effect estimates, $H^1 \neq 0$ reduces to significant Cochran's $Q$ (Appendix~\ref{app:sheaf-cochran}), which is equivalent to testing whether the selection variables indexing the strata are ignorable for transport.
The cohomological framing adds no power over standard random-effects meta-analysis in this case; its value is conceptual (connecting heterogeneity testing to the formal transport literature) and extensional (the per-edge generalization, which has no standard-statistics analogue).

\subsection{Ancestry heterogeneity and the LD confound}
\label{sec:ld-confound}

A natural concern with the $H^1$ classification on ancestry-stratified MR estimates is that the detected heterogeneity may reflect linkage disequilibrium (LD) differences, allele frequency variation, or instrument validity rather than causal effect modification.
Two features of the results argue against LD as the sole driver.
First, all 39 transport pairs also span the same ancestry strata (European, East Asian, African, Hispanic) yet produce uniformly low $Q$ values ($\leq 0.70$); if LD differences alone inflated $Q$, transport pairs would also show elevated heterogeneity.
Second, the cardiometabolic domain---which involves entirely different genetic architectures from the HLA-dominated neurological pairs---achieves 100\% accuracy (18/18), ruling out an HLA-specific LD artifact as the explanation for the overall result.
A simulation calibrates the magnitude of LD-attributable heterogeneity.
We model 4 ancestry strata with constant causal effect ($\beta_{\mathrm{causal}}$ fixed) but varying IV--exposure association strength ($\gamma_k \sim N(\gamma_0, \sigma_{\mathrm{LD}}^2)$) to capture LD-driven denominator variation in Wald ratios.
Sweeping relative LD variation $\sigma_{\mathrm{LD}}/\gamma_0$ from 0 to 0.30 (500 replicates per level), the resulting $Q$ distribution has median 0.29 at $\sigma_{\mathrm{LD}}/\gamma_0 = 0.05$ and median 0.58 at 0.10---matching the observed transport-pair $Q$ distribution (median 0.40, mean 0.44).
Even at $\sigma_{\mathrm{LD}}/\gamma_0 = 0.30$ (extreme LD variation, well beyond empirical estimates), only $13.4\%$ of replicates exceed $Q > 7$, and the median is 2.66---far below the observed non-transport median of 12.0.
The 95th percentile at $\sigma_{\mathrm{LD}}/\gamma_0 = 0.20$ reaches 5.15, still below the non-transport range.
These results confirm that: (1)~transport-pair $Q$ values are consistent with moderate LD variation ($\sigma_{\mathrm{LD}}/\gamma_0 \approx 0.05$--$0.10$); (2)~non-transport $Q$ values ($\text{median} = 12.0$) are not reachable under any plausible LD-only model, requiring genuine causal effect modification or instrument invalidity to explain.

\subsection{Clinical implications}

The MS and AD results illustrate how edge-specific heterogeneity arises naturally in clinical settings.
Both diseases have multi-process causal architectures that generate edge-specific heterogeneity invisible to global tests.
Transportability assessment---which effects generalize across patient strata---should be a standard step before pooling multi-site estimates.

The real-data validation on 61 MR pairs confirms that these applications extend beyond neurodegeneration.
Cardiometabolic, cancer, and psychiatric MR pairs all follow the same pattern: transport pairs have $Q < 1$, non-transport pairs with adequate power have $Q > 7$, and false positives do not occur. The $H^1$ classifier requires no domain-specific tuning---the same $\alpha = 0.05$ threshold works across all five domains.

\subsection{Limitations}

The simulations are existence demonstrations with planted structure, not realistic clinical models: confounding is complete (all-or-nothing), SEMs use random DAGs without domain structure, and heterogeneity patterns (mechanism-edge variance $100\times$ larger than downstream) are set by design.
The MS and AD simulations share identical coefficient values with different labels (Tables~\ref{tab:ms-sem} and~\ref{tab:ad-sem}); the AD simulation confirms pipeline consistency on matched inputs, not cross-domain generalization. Cross-domain evidence comes from the 61-pair real-data validation (\S\ref{sec:realdata}), where effect sizes and stratum counts are determined by published studies.
Real clinical data present partial confounding, measurement error, missing data, and continuous rather than discrete variation.
All drug-effect coefficients (BTK, siponimod, lecanemab) are simulated values calibrated to clinical expectations, not estimated from trial data.

The real-data validation (\S\ref{sec:realdata}) uses published summary statistics rather than individual-level data, so the $Q$ test operates on 3--4 strata rather than the 8+ in simulation.
This limits power: the nine misclassified pairs all have power $< 0.38$ at $\alpha = 0.05$.
Individual-level replication with $\geq 8$ population strata would test whether the simulation's high power ($> 0.95$) holds.

The $H^1$ classifier's scale-invariance stress test did not fully pass in either domain: quantile normalization reduced scale variability (MS: $0.517 \to 0.346$; AD: $0.734 \to 0.695$) but neither reached the $0.30$ target.
Developing scale-free alternatives to OLS-based sheaf $Q$ tests is an open direction.

The real-data $H^1$ classification uses ancestry strata from different published studies, so heterogeneity reflects a composite of causal effect modification, LD structure, allele frequency, instrument validity, and demographic differences.
The classification tests exchangeability, not effect modification in isolation.
Standard instrument-level MR sensitivity analyses (MR-Egger, weighted median, Steiger filtering) require individual SNP-level data and test a different hypothesis (instrument pleiotropy) than the stratum-level $Q$ test (population exchangeability); the stratum-level robustness checks reported in \S\ref{sec:realdata-stratum-robustness} are the appropriate sensitivity analyses for this data structure.

The confound audit recovers the correct metric ordering but cannot detect suppressor effects from published additive variance decompositions.
Detecting non-additive confounding requires individual-level multivariate data.

%% ==============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ==============================================================

Per-edge sheaf $Q$ tests and $H^1$ transportability classification provide a practical workflow for assessing which Mendelian randomization results generalize across populations. On scalar effect estimates, the sheaf $Q$ test reduces algebraically to Cochran's $Q$; the contribution is the per-edge extension and the $H^1$ classification framework, which have no standard meta-analytic counterpart.

On 61 published MR estimates across five clinical domains, the $H^1$ classifier achieves 85.2\% accuracy [75.4\%, 93.4\%] at $\alpha = 0.05$ with zero false positives, rising to 90.2\% at $\alpha = 0.10$.
All nine failures are false negatives with power $< 0.38$; correctly detected pairs have power $> 0.62$, with no pairs in between.
The expansion from 17 neuro-specific pairs to 61 pairs spanning cardiometabolic disease, cancer, and psychiatric disorders confirms that the classification is not domain-specific, though the classifier tests exchangeability of stratum-level estimates (a composite of effect modification, LD, and instrument validity), not causal effect modification in isolation (\S\ref{sec:ld-confound}).
Per-edge tests on ADNI longitudinal data recover a three-way DAG classification (mechanism-switching, mediator dose-response, stable bypass) richer than the simulation's binary prediction.

\subsubsection*{Broader Impact Statement}
This work applies sheaf consistency testing to classify Mendelian randomization transportability and recover causal DAG structure. The methods identified here are diagnostic: they tell practitioners when effect estimates are exchangeable across populations. Misapplication of heterogeneity tests to underpowered data risks false confidence in transportability assessments, which could affect evidence synthesis and clinical guideline development. The power analysis explicitly characterizes the regime where the classifier fails, providing actionable guidance. No direct negative societal impact is anticipated from this methodological contribution.

The real-data validation uses published summary statistics (Appendix~\ref{app:mr-sources}) and does not require individual-level data access. Random seeds are fixed for all stochastic simulations.

\section{Sheaf $Q$ reduces to Cochran's $Q$}
\label{app:sheaf-cochran}

We show that on a complete graph with scalar stalks and pairwise-difference restriction maps, the sheaf Laplacian test statistic (Eq.~\ref{eq:sheaf-Q}) is algebraically equivalent to Cochran's $Q$ for random-effects meta-analysis.

\paragraph{Setup.}
Let $K$ sites report scalar estimates $\hat\beta_1, \ldots, \hat\beta_K$ with variances $\sigma_k^2 = \mathrm{se}_k^2$. Define inverse-variance weights $w_k = 1/\sigma_k^2$ and the weighted mean $\bar\beta = \sum_k w_k \hat\beta_k / \sum_k w_k$.

\paragraph{Cochran's $Q$.}
The standard form is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cochran}
    Q_C = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k (\hat\beta_k - \bar\beta)^2.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Sheaf Laplacian form.}
On the complete graph $K_K$, the signed incidence matrix $D_0 \in \R^{\binom{K}{2} \times K}$ has one row per edge $(i,j)$ with $+1$ at position $i$ and $-1$ at position $j$. The obstruction vector is $\mathbf{o} = D_0 \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$, so $o_{ij} = \hat\beta_i - \hat\beta_j$. The covariance matrix is $\Sigma = D_0 \operatorname{diag}(\sigma^2) D_0^\top$, with entries $\Sigma_{(ij),(ij)} = \sigma_i^2 + \sigma_j^2$ on the diagonal and $\Sigma_{(ij),(ik)} = \sigma_i^2$ for edges sharing vertex $i$.

\paragraph{Equivalence.}
The sheaf test statistic is $Q_S = \mathbf{o}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{o}$. Because $\mathbf{o} = D_0 \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ lies in $\mathrm{im}(D_0) = (\ker D_0^\top)^\perp$, and $\Sigma = D_0 W^{-1} D_0^\top$ where $W = \operatorname{diag}(w_1, \ldots, w_K)$, the quadratic form reduces to
\begin{align}
    Q_S &= \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}^\top D_0^\top (D_0 W^{-1} D_0^\top)^{-1} D_0 \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \nonumber \\
        &= \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}^\top (W - W \mathbf{1}({\mathbf{1}}^\top W \mathbf{1})^{-1} {\mathbf{1}}^\top W) \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \label{eq:pseudoinverse} \\
        &= \sum_k w_k \hat\beta_k^2 - \frac{(\sum_k w_k \hat\beta_k)^2}{\sum_k w_k} \nonumber \\
        &= \sum_k w_k (\hat\beta_k - \bar\beta)^2 = Q_C. \nonumber
\end{align}

The key step (Eq.~\ref{eq:pseudoinverse}) uses the fact that $D_0^\top (D_0 W^{-1} D_0^\top)^{-1} D_0 = W - W \mathbf{1}(\mathbf{1}^\top W \mathbf{1})^{-1} \mathbf{1}^\top W$, which is the projection onto the complement of the constant vector in the $W$-weighted inner product. This holds because $\ker(D_0) = \mathrm{span}(\mathbf{1})$ for a connected graph, so $D_0^\top \Sigma^{-1} D_0$ is the Schur complement that projects out the mean.

Under the null hypothesis of no between-site heterogeneity, both $Q_C$ and $Q_S$ follow $\chi^2_{K-1}$.
